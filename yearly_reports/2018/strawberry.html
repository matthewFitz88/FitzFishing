Summer

Kokanee

  • Trolling with a Silver Dodger

Cutthroat

  • Trolling with a Silver Dodger
  • Trolling with 4 bladed silver or gold willow leaf pop gear
  • Trolling with 5 bladed Colorado multi-color pop gear
  • Trolling, casting, and jigging 2.5 to 3" white tube jigs

Trolling Tips

  • Trolling Speed: 1 to 1.5MPH.
  • All our pop gear was trailed with either a worm or pink or green squids.
  • We usually would tip our squids with pink maggots to add flavor and scent.
  • Add weight or use down riggers to get to deeper fish, if needed.

Jig Trolling Tips

  • Trolling Speed: 2 to 3MPH.
  • Best for early summer when fish are still high in the water column.

Jig Casting Tips

  • We always tipped our jigs with Chub meat for extra scent and flavor to draw fish in.
  • The most common retrieve that we would use for the jigs would be a porpoising retrieve where you would cast out your jig, let it sink, and then begin jigging your rod up and then reeling back down and repeating the process for the entire cast.
  • The way you'll know if you get a hit is when you go to jig it will feel a little bit more heavy than normal and this is because the Trout will normally just grab the jig as it sinks after the jig so you usually don't feel the hit.
  • If you feel a hit then you need to either quickly set the hook or reel down very quickly and then set the hook so the fish doesn't have time to let go of the jig before you can hook them.
  • If you set the hook and miss, don't give up. Reel up some of the slack and let the jig sink for a few seconds, depending on how deep of water you're in, and watch to see if you line either doesn't sink or slacks off as it's sinking. If either of these happen, reel up the remaining slack and set the hook because it means that the fish has hit again.
  • Once your jig comes back into view, watch closely what's going on because sometimes that Trout will follow the jig all the way in. So, if you see a fish following your jig and it hasn't spooked off yet but you're running out of distance to the boat, let your jig sink so that the fish doesn't see the boat and then jig try to jig and get the to bite that way.
  • If you get to the end of your cast and fish is following and you miss it or it turns away on the jig. Don't give up on that fish, watch which direction it swims off too and then flip your jig out in that direction and let it sink for a few seconds and usually the fish will come back and hit it or chase it again.
  • For a visual way of seeing how this casting method should be done please watch the video at the bottom of the page.

Jigging Tips

  • Most easily done with a fish finder
  • If the fish is aggressively following your jig as you jig it, then perform around 3 jigs in a row and then dead-stick the jig to get a hit.
  • If the fish is not aggressively following your jig then dead-stick the jig or slow your jigging down to see if that triggers a bite.
  • If you get hit and miss, don't give up, just lower your rod tip again and let the jig sink back down, and usually this will trigger another bite once the jig is done sinking back down.
  • If your jig is supposed to be sinking back down, but isn't or your line is slack, this means that a fish has grabbed onto your jig as it was sinking, so at this point, reel up the slack and set the hook.
  • If you're near the bottom, sometimes bottom bouncing your jig can also trigger a bite.

Fall

Best Strategies

  • Tolling
  • 2.5 to 3" white tube jigs
  • Casting orange and gold Kamloopers
  • Casting silver and gold Pot-O-Gold's

Fall Tips

  • For trolling and tube jigs, use the same strategies mentioned above.
  • Best to use spoons when it's calm and you see fish hitting the surface.

Ice Fishing

Best Lures

  • 2.5 to 3" white tube jigs
  • 1" green tube jigs

Tips

  • We always tipped our bigger jigs with Chub meat and our smaller jigs with wax worms or pink maggots for extra scent and flavor to draw fish in.
  • We will also usually keep our smaller jigs at the top of the water column, about 15' or less, so that higher running fish can still be caught. While our lager jigs are usually kept deeper.
  • A lot of ice fishing is looking at the fish finder and reading what the fish are doing and how they are acting around your lure and what you do with it. Sometimes some fish will want it moving a lot and other times they don't want it moving at all, and there really isn't a way of telling which it'll be most of the time, so you will just have to experiment until you figure out which they like more.
  • If the fish is aggressively following your jig as you jig it, then perform around 3 jigs in a row and then dead-stick the jig to get a hit.
